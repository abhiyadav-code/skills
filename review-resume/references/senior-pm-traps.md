# Senior PM Resume Traps

Load this reference when evaluating senior, staff, or principal PM roles (or equivalent senior IC roles in other functions). The four mistakes below are from Alex Rechevskiy's resume coaching framework — they are the most common reasons strong senior PMs fail to land interviews even when their substantive fit is high.

Apply this lens explicitly in the "Top callback killers" and "Strengths to lean into" sections of the output.

## Mistake #1 — Weak top third

**The problem**: Recruiters spend 7–10 seconds scanning a resume top-down. If the first few lines don't grab them, they never reach the experience section.

**What candidates do wrong**:
- Leading with a generic objective statement
- Burying recognizable company names below the fold
- Starting with a "skills" section full of buzzwords
- Writing a 3-line summary maximum filled with empty descriptors

**The fix**:
- Lead with the biggest anchor: recognizable company, impressive number, or specific expertise
- Keep it to 1–2 lines max
- Think billboard, not biography
- Test: "Would a recruiter stop scrolling?"

**Before/after example**:
- Before: "Results-driven product leader with 8+ years of experience seeking a challenging role where I can leverage my skills in stakeholder management, roadmap development, and cross-functional collaboration."
- After: "Ex-Google PM | $50M+ revenue impact | AI/ML products | Led 3 product teams"

## Mistake #2 — Responsibilities instead of impact

**The problem**: Hiring managers aren't looking for a list of things you did. They're looking for what happened *because* you did them.

**What candidates do wrong**:
- Describing job duties instead of results
- Using verbs like "managed," "coordinated," "supported," "responsible for"
- Missing numbers entirely
- Telling the reader what they were *responsible for*, not what they *delivered*

**The fix**:
- Every bullet should answer "So what? What was the result?"
- Add numbers: revenue, users, % improvement, time saved
- Use impact verbs: "Drove," "Increased," "Launched," "Reduced"
- Format: [Action] + [What you did] + [Quantified result]

**Before/after example**:
- Before: "Managed product roadmap and coordinated with engineering teams on feature delivery. Responsible for stakeholder communication and sprint planning."
- After: "Increased user retention 40% by redesigning onboarding flow — drove $3M incremental ARR in 6 months."

## Mistake #3 — Hand-wavy generic statements

**The problem**: Vague statements sound impressive but communicate nothing. Recruiters can't evaluate what they can't understand.

**What candidates do wrong**:
- Using corporate buzzwords that mean nothing specific
- Being vague about scope, scale, and results
- Hiding behind phrases that could apply to anyone
- Not explaining what their company or product actually did

**The fix**:
- Replace every vague phrase with specifics: What? How many? What result?
- Name the product, the metric, the outcome
- If a recruiter can't picture what you did, rewrite it
- Test: "Could 1,000 other PMs write this exact bullet?" If yes, it's too generic.

**Before/after example**:
- Before: "Performed extensive market research. Drove key strategic initiatives. Partnered cross-functionally to deliver results. Owned end-to-end product development."
- After: "Conducted 47 user interviews across 3 segments → identified pricing gap → launched new tier that drove $2M ARR. Led 0→1 launch of fraud detection product — reduced chargebacks 32% for enterprise clients."

## Mistake #4 — Walls of text that get skipped

**The problem**: Long bullet points intimidate readers. If your bullet doesn't get read, it doesn't help you get the callback.

**What candidates do wrong**:
- Writing 3–4 line bullet points
- Cramming multiple accomplishments into one bullet
- Using run-on sentences with semicolons
- Creating dense paragraphs with no white space

**The fix**:
- 1–2 lines per bullet, max
- One story per bullet — if two accomplishments, make two bullets
- Use white space strategically — let the eye breathe
- Front-load the impact — put the result first, context second

**Before/after example**:
- Before: "Led cross-functional team of engineers, designers, and data scientists to develop and launch new customer-facing dashboard product that integrated with existing platform and required coordination across three business units while also managing stakeholder expectations and presenting quarterly updates to executive leadership."
- After: "Launched customer dashboard product — 15K MAU in 90 days. Aligned 3 business units on shared roadmap; presented quarterly to VP+. Led cross-functional team of 8 (eng, design, data science)."

## The Callback Checklist

Before scoring a senior PM resume, ask:

- Does the top third have a recognizable anchor? (Mistake #1)
- Does every bullet show impact, not just responsibility? (Mistake #2)
- Could 1,000 other PMs write any of these bullets verbatim? If yes, they're too generic. (Mistake #3)
- Are all bullets 1–2 lines and scannable? (Mistake #4)
- Would a recruiter spending 7 seconds see the candidate's best stuff?

If the answer to any of these is "no," call it out specifically in the evaluation.

## How to apply this in a review

- In the **callback killers section**, name the specific Mistake # when relevant. Recruiters and PM coaches know this framework; the candidate may too. Naming it shows you're applying a real heuristic, not vibes.
- In **bullet rewrites**, follow the [Action] + [What you did] + [Quantified result] pattern.
- For **strengths to lean into**, identify where the resume *does* avoid these traps (e.g. a strong outcome bullet) and tell the candidate to apply the same pattern to weaker bullets.

## Beware the under-leveling trap

Particularly for senior/staff/principal roles, watch for resume choices that signal a lower level:

- Bullets describing feature-level work instead of program/portfolio-level outcomes
- Using "I" framing when the role expects influence across teams
- Listing individual deliverables instead of business outcomes
- Title language that reads as IC when the role expects leadership scope (or vice versa)

If the user is at a Big Tech company where PM titles are flat (Google PM, Meta PM) but maps to senior/staff levels elsewhere, suggest adding a parenthetical level equivalent (e.g. "Product Manager (L6 / Senior Staff equivalent)") — or trust that the company name signals level. The choice depends on the reader; for non-Big-Tech recruiters, the parenthetical helps.
